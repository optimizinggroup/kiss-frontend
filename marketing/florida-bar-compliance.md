# Florida Bar Compliance Reference — KISS Marketing

> ⚠️ **Not legal advice.** This document is an operational reference based on public Florida Bar rules. It is NOT a substitute for review by a Florida-licensed attorney. Before any KISS-driven marketing communication goes live under an attorney's brand, that attorney (or their compliance counsel) must review the materials and confirm they meet current Bar standards. Rules and interpretations change.

## The rule set that applies

Florida Bar **Rules of Professional Conduct, Chapter 4-7 — Information About Legal Services** (commonly called "the advertising rules") governs every commercial communication that promotes legal services. KISS-generated emails, the policy review report itself, and the branded URL all fall under this umbrella because they're commercial communications about an attorney's services.

The most relevant rules for KISS:

- **Rule 4-7.11** — Application. Defines what counts as advertising; KISS communications do.
- **Rule 4-7.12** — Required content. Every advertisement must include the name of the lawyer or law firm and at least one bona fide office location.
- **Rule 4-7.13** — Misleading or deceptive content. Cannot create unjustified expectations, cannot use unverifiable claims, cannot compare to other lawyers without factual substantiation.
- **Rule 4-7.14** — Potentially misleading content. Things like dramatizations, stock images of clients, testimonials require specific disclosures.
- **Rule 4-7.15** — Unduly manipulative. No fear-based marketing tactics, no manipulative imagery.
- **Rule 4-7.16** — Presumptively valid content. Safe-harbor list of what's clearly OK.
- **Rule 4-7.18** — Direct contact / solicitation. The big one for nurture emails. Restricts unsolicited contact with prospective clients.
- **Rule 4-7.19** — Filing requirement. Many advertisements must be filed with the Florida Bar's Ethics & Advertising Department within 20 days of first dissemination, with a $150 filing fee, unless an exemption applies.
- **Rule 4-7.21** — Firm names and letterheads.
- **Rule 4-7.22** — Referrals to and from non-lawyers.

## How KISS communications fit

| Communication | What it is | Most relevant rules |
|---|---|---|
| Branded landing page (`kiss.optimizinggroup.com/{slug}`) | Advertisement of legal services | 4-7.12, 4-7.13, 4-7.14, 4-7.16, 4-7.19 (filing) |
| Free policy review report (HTML email) | Provided service + advertisement | 4-7.13 (no unjustified expectations), 4-7.14, 4-7.16 |
| Nurture-sequence emails (Days 7/30/60/90) | Direct contact with someone who's already engaged | 4-7.18 (solicitation), 4-7.13 |
| Sales materials shown to attorney prospects | NOT under 4-7 (that's about attorney→consumer); under FTC + general truth-in-advertising | N/A from a Bar standpoint |

## The opt-in matters — a lot

Rule 4-7.18 is heavily restrictive of *unsolicited* contact with prospective clients. KISS sidesteps the most aggressive parts of this rule because **the homeowner voluntarily initiated the relationship** by visiting the branded URL, filling out the form, and uploading their policy. That's an inbound, consensual interaction — not the kind of cold outreach 4-7.18 was written to prohibit.

But this only holds if you preserve the consensual nature throughout:

1. **Clear consent at intake.** The form must include a checkbox like: "I agree to receive my AI-powered policy review and follow-up educational emails from [Attorney/Firm Name]. I understand this does not create an attorney-client relationship and that I can unsubscribe at any time."
2. **Easy opt-out.** Every email must have a one-click unsubscribe link.
3. **Don't share or sell the email list.** The homeowner consented to communications from THIS attorney. They didn't consent to be on a marketing list resold to other firms.
4. **Don't escalate to phone calls or texts** without explicit additional consent. Email-only is the safest channel here.

If you do those four things, the nurture sequence falls into the "communications with someone who has expressed interest" zone, which is presumptively allowed.

## Required content in every email

Every nurture-sequence email must contain, at minimum:

- **Name of the law firm or attorney** sending the email (Rule 4-7.12)
- **Bona fide office address** (Rule 4-7.12) — physical address, not just a P.O. box
- **A statement that this is advertising / commercial communication** — typical placement is a tagline at the bottom such as *"This is an advertisement for legal services."* — required for direct mail under 4-7.18(b), best practice for email even where not strictly required.
- **Disclaimer that this does not create an attorney-client relationship.**
- **Disclaimer that AI-generated content is informational only and not legal advice.**
- **Easy unsubscribe mechanism.**
- **The reviewing attorney's Florida Bar number** is best practice (not strictly required for advertising, but helps establish legitimacy).

## What cannot appear in any KISS communication

Things that will get an attorney into trouble fast:

- "Specialist" or "expert" in any practice area, **unless** the attorney is actually board certified by the Florida Bar in that specialty (Rule 4-7.14(a)(4))
- Promises or guarantees of outcomes ("we'll win your case", "you'll get the maximum recovery")
- Comparisons to other lawyers without factual substantiation ("the best PI firm in Miami" is not OK)
- Past results presented in a way that creates unjustified expectations about future cases (a single case result without context is risky)
- Testimonials from clients without the required disclaimer about non-typical results
- Dramatizations of legal scenarios without a disclosure
- Direct fear-based language ("you'll lose everything if you don't call us") — Rule 4-7.15

## Filing requirement (Rule 4-7.19)

Most advertisements directed to a prospective client in Florida must be filed with the Florida Bar's Ethics & Advertising Department for review, accompanied by a $150 filing fee, **within 20 days of first dissemination** — unless an exemption applies.

**Exemptions that may apply to KISS:**

- Communications to existing clients (4-7.16 — once a homeowner uses the form, an argument exists they've initiated a professional relationship — verify with FL counsel)
- Tombstone information (firm name, address, contact info, areas of practice, in plain text)
- Information on the lawyer's own website that's not an active solicitation

**Practically, plan to file the following with the Bar's Advertising Department:**

- The branded landing page template (one filing covers the template; per-attorney variants are typically considered iterations)
- Each of the 5 nurture-sequence emails
- The "free policy review available" promotional copy that the attorney uses to drive traffic to the branded URL

Budget: $150 × 7 communications × number of attorneys = call it $1,000-$2,000 per attorney for the initial compliance review pass. Some of this is shared (template-level) and shouldn't recur per-attorney.

## Florida-specific recent context

Florida insurance law changed materially in 2022-2023 (SB 2A, HB 7065, AOB reform). These changes affect:

- One-way attorney fee statutes for first-party property claims (significantly altered)
- Assignment of Benefits restrictions
- Bad-faith timing requirements
- Hurricane deductible structures

**Any KISS marketing copy that references "we'll fight for what you're owed" or similar must be reviewed against the current statutory landscape.** What was true marketing copy in 2021 may be misleading in 2026. This is the single most important thing for FL counsel to verify.

## Pre-launch compliance checklist

Before activating any branded KISS deployment for a Florida attorney:

- [ ] Florida-licensed attorney has reviewed every public-facing communication (landing page, intake form copy, report disclaimers, all 5 nurture emails)
- [ ] The reviewing attorney has confirmed it complies with current Chapter 4-7 rules
- [ ] Filing fees paid and confirmation received from FL Bar Ethics & Advertising Dept for required filings
- [ ] Each communication contains: firm name, physical address, advertising disclosure, "not legal advice" disclaimer, "no attorney-client relationship created" disclaimer, unsubscribe
- [ ] Intake form contains explicit consent checkbox covering the policy review AND ongoing email communications
- [ ] Unsubscribe mechanism tested end-to-end (Resend handles this natively if configured)
- [ ] Email-events table in Supabase includes a `consent_status` field on every contact record
- [ ] No language anywhere references "specialist" / "expert" unless the attorney is board certified
- [ ] No language anywhere promises or guarantees outcomes
- [ ] Comparative claims about other firms are removed or factually substantiated with citation
- [ ] Bar number of reviewing attorney is included in email signatures (best practice)

## Sample compliant disclaimer language

**Footer block for every email and the report itself:**

> ADVERTISING DISCLOSURE: This is an advertisement for legal services from [Firm Name]. The information provided in this communication is for general informational and educational purposes only and is not legal advice. No attorney-client relationship is created by your use of this tool, by receipt of this report, or by reading this email. Past results do not guarantee similar outcomes in future cases. AI-generated analysis is provided as a convenience and does not replace consultation with a licensed attorney. To stop receiving these emails, click [unsubscribe link]. [Firm Name], [Bona Fide Office Address], [Florida Bar Number of Responsible Attorney].

**Consent checkbox on the intake form:**

> ☐ I am requesting a complimentary AI-generated review of my insurance policy from [Firm Name]. I understand this is informational only, not legal advice, and does not create an attorney-client relationship. I consent to receive my report and educational follow-up emails from [Firm Name]. I can unsubscribe at any time.

## What to do when an attorney prospect asks "is this Bar-compliant?"

The honest, accurate answer is: **"The KISS framework is built around Florida Bar Chapter 4-7, but ultimate compliance is your responsibility as the advertising attorney. We provide compliant template language, an opt-in intake flow, and required disclaimer placement. You — or your compliance counsel — should review the specific rollout for your firm and file required communications with the Bar before going live. We can help you assemble the filing package."**

That's accurate, doesn't overpromise, and shifts the responsibility appropriately. Selling KISS as a turnkey "we handle compliance" product is dangerous for both you and the attorney.

## Out of scope for this document

- States other than Florida — every state bar has its own advertising rules. Some are more permissive (Texas), some are more restrictive (Florida is on the strict end). Before expanding to a new state, do this same exercise for that state's rules.
- Attorney-client privilege issues if homeowner-uploaded PDFs contain privileged material — they generally won't, but worth a note.
- Insurance fraud / referral kickback issues — KISS is a marketing tool, not a referral service, so 4-7.22 (referrals from nonlawyers) likely doesn't apply, but verify with counsel.
