```
Hi {{contact_name}},

Your complimentary property insurance policy review is ready. The full breakdown is below —
what you're covered for, what's excluded, and where the gaps are.

Three things every Florida property owner should know before reading the report:

1. Most Florida homeowners are underinsured at rebuild time. Florida rebuild costs run
   $200-250 per square foot in 2026 dollars. If your dwelling coverage is below that math,
   you'd cover the difference out of pocket if your home was destroyed. Your report calculates
   this for your specific property.

2. Flood is almost always excluded from a standard homeowners policy. If you don't have a
   separate NFIP or private flood policy, you're not covered for rising water damage — even in
   a hurricane. Wind-driven rain is sometimes covered, sometimes not, depending on your
   policy's wording. The distinction matters.

3. Hurricane deductibles are separate and usually a percentage of your dwelling coverage,
   often 2-5%. On a $400,000 dwelling, that's $8,000-$20,000 you'd pay before insurance starts.
   Most owners don't realize this until they file a claim.
```

## Email 2 — Day 7 — "Photographing your home: what to capture before a storm"

**Sent:** 7 days after Email 1.
**Subject:** `Photograph your home today (here's how)`
**Preheader:** `30 minutes now saves weeks of frustration if a claim happens`

```
Hi {{contact_name}},

Quick follow-up to your policy review last week.

If you ever have to file a property insurance claim, the single most useful evidence you'll
have is photos taken BEFORE damage happens. Adjusters compare your "before" to your "after."
If you don't have "before," they assume the damage was pre-existing or exaggerated.

Spend 30 minutes this week walking your property with your phone. Here's the shot list:

EXTERIOR:
- All four sides of the house from 20 feet back
- Roof from the ground (every angle you can see)
- Any visible damage you currently know about (an old scratch, a cracked tile) — date-stamp
  these so they're clearly pre-existing
- Fences, sheds, pool cage, screen enclosure
- Trees near the house (these become claim issues after storms)
- AC units and pool equipment
- Driveway, walkways, and any landscape features

INTERIOR (every room):
- All four walls of every room from each corner
- Open closets briefly (proves contents)
- Open kitchen cabinets briefly
- Photograph appliances with model numbers visible
- Photograph any high-value items: art, jewelry, electronics, designer items, collections
- Capture the receipts/serial numbers if you have them

DOCUMENTS:
- Photograph your declarations page (the cover page of your policy showing limits)
- Photograph your most recent bill / proof of premium paid
- Photograph any home improvement receipts (kitchen reno, new roof, etc.)

Where to store all this:
- Cloud backup (iCloud, Google Photos, Dropbox) so it survives even if your phone is lost
- Email a copy to yourself (creates a date-stamped trail)
- If commercial property: also store outside the building (off-site backup)

This sounds tedious. Most people skip it until after their first claim, when they wish they
hadn't.

— {{tenant.contact_name}}
{{tenant.brand_name}}

[Compliance footer]
```

**Why this email:** Genuinely useful, evergreen, action-oriented. Plants the firm as the practical resource. The implicit message: "we know what claims need; we represent claimants."

## Email 3 — Day 30 — "What to do in the first 48 hours after damage"

**Sent:** 30 days after Email 1.
**Subject:** `The first 48 hours after property damage matter most`
**Preheader:** `A practical playbook from {{tenant.brand_name}}`

```
Hi {{contact_name}},

It's been about a month since your policy review. Here's something every Florida property
owner should have on hand — what to do in the first 48 hours after storm damage, water
damage, fire, or break-in.

Hour 0-2 — Safety first:
1. Get everyone safe. If structural damage, gas smell, electrical issues — leave and call
   utilities/911.
2. Stop additional damage if you safely can. Tarp a roof. Shut off water at the main. Move
   contents away from leaks. Florida policies require "reasonable efforts to mitigate further
   damage" — failing to do this can reduce a claim payout.

Hour 2-12 — Document everything:
3. Photograph and video EVERYTHING. The damage, the cause if visible (fallen tree, broken
   pipe), the path of damage through the property, every wet wall, every ruined item.
4. Take more photos than you think you need. 200 photos of a damaged kitchen are not too
   many.
5. Don't throw anything away yet, no matter how ruined. Insurance companies want to see
   damaged contents. Pile damaged items in one area but don't dispose until your adjuster
   has seen them.

Hour 12-24 — Notify and engage your insurer:
6. Call your insurance company's claims line. Get a claim number. Write down the claims
   adjuster's name and direct line.
7. Request a copy of your policy in writing. They have to provide this. You'd be surprised
   how often the policy you "have" is different from the policy your insurer says you have.
8. Start a daily log: every conversation with the insurer (date, time, name, what was said),
   every step you take, every contractor estimate you get.

Hour 24-48 — Get independent estimates:
9. Get at least two independent repair estimates from licensed Florida contractors. Do NOT
   rely solely on the insurer's preferred contractors — they work for the insurer.
10. Get a public adjuster involved if the claim is significant. They work on contingency and
    represent YOUR interests, not the insurer's. (Different from a lawyer; complementary
    role.)

What NOT to do:
- Don't sign anything from the insurance company in the first 48 hours without reading every
  word, especially anything labeled "release," "settlement," or "AOB" (Assignment of Benefits
  has been heavily restricted in FL since 2019 — confirm any AOB request with counsel first).
- Don't accept the first offer. Initial offers are almost always low.
- Don't let the insurer's adjuster write off damage they didn't see. If they missed a room or
  an item, document it and demand a re-inspection.
- Don't post damage on social media before discussing with your attorney. Insurers monitor
  social.

If you ever find yourself in a denied or underpaid claim situation, that's the moment to call
us. The first 48 hours of a denial — like the first 48 hours of damage — set the trajectory
of what happens next.

Reply to this email or call {{tenant.phone}} if you need a second pair of eyes on a claim.
Free consult, no obligation.

— {{tenant.contact_name}}
{{tenant.brand_name}}

[Compliance footer]
```

**Why this email:** Highest-value content. Genuinely useful playbook. Naturally introduces the firm as the resource for a denied or underpaid claim — the moment a property attorney actually converts.

## Email 4 — Day 60 — "Hurricane season prep checklist"

**Sent:** 60 days after Email 1. Time of year matters here — if Day 60 falls between February and May, this email is perfectly timed (heading into hurricane season). If it falls outside that window, the content still works but the framing should adapt.

**Subject:** `Hurricane season checklist — is your coverage ready?`
**Preheader:** `7 things to do before June 1`

```
Hi {{contact_name}},

Hurricane season runs June 1 through November 30. Whatever month you're reading this, here's
a 7-item checklist worth running through:

1. Pull your declarations page out and re-read it.
   Your dwelling coverage limit, hurricane deductible (usually a percentage), and any
   exclusions are the three things that matter most when a claim hits. Most homeowners haven't
   looked at theirs in years.

2. Know your hurricane deductible in actual dollars.
   "2% of dwelling coverage" sounds abstract. On a $500,000 dwelling, that's $10,000. Have
   that money set aside or know where you'd get it from in an emergency. After Hurricane Ian
   in 2022, many Florida homeowners discovered they couldn't access this gap money for weeks.

3. Verify you have flood insurance if you need it.
   Standard homeowners policies do NOT cover flood. NFIP coverage takes 30 days to take
   effect after purchase — you cannot wait until a storm is forecast. Private flood is
   sometimes faster but more expensive.

4. Run a wind mitigation inspection if you haven't recently.
   Florida law requires insurers to give discounts for wind-mitigation features (impact
   windows, hip roof, roof-deck attachment, etc.). Many homeowners qualify and don't claim
   the discount because they've never had the inspection. The inspection costs $75-150; the
   savings can be hundreds per year.

5. Photograph your home interior and exterior now (if you didn't after Email #2).
   See last month's email for the full photo checklist. Without "before" photos, "after"
   claims become much harder.

6. Trim trees within 10 feet of your roof.
   Tree damage is a common storm claim AND a common claim denial — insurers argue homeowner
   negligence if a clearly-overhanging branch causes damage.

7. Inventory contents over $1,000.
   Receipts, serial numbers, photos. Many policies cap individual items (jewelry, art,
   electronics) at $1,000-2,500 unless specifically scheduled. Higher-value items often need a
   rider you don't have.

If you'd like a fresh policy review now that hurricane season is in view, reply to this email
and we'll run another one through KISS. No charge.

Stay safe out there.

— {{tenant.contact_name}}
{{tenant.brand_name}}

[Compliance footer]
```

**Why this email:** Hurricane season is THE conversion trigger for property attorneys in FL. This email is the practical preparation guide that primes the homeowner to think of the firm when something happens.

## Variant: Commercial property emails

When `policy_type` indicates commercial (BOP, CGL, Special Form Property), Emails 2-5 should swap a few details:

- Replace "homeowner" with "property owner" or "business owner"
- Email 2 (photos): add "exterior signage, equipment, leasehold improvements, customer-facing spaces"
- Email 3 (first 48 hrs): add Business Interruption (BI) coverage as a separate consideration — "document lost revenue from day one, contemporaneous records matter"
- Email 4 (hurricane prep): substitute "business continuity plan" framing — what happens to your operations if the building is uninhabitable for 90 days?
- Email 5 (annual): mention business growth, new locations, new equipment, new lines of business

Recommend: keep the residential and commercial sequences as separate templates in Make. Trigger the commercial variant when `policy_type IN ('BOP','CGL','Special Form','Commercial Property')`. The Make scenario can do this with a router branch on policy_type.

## What's NOT in this sequence (intentional)

- No "we'll fight for what you're owed" claim language. Florida's 2022-2023 insurance reform changed the legal landscape; aggressive claim-recovery promises need verification by FL counsel against current law.
- No specific dollar recoveries or case results. Past results in FL marketing must be carefully framed under 4-7.13/14.
- No "you're definitely underinsured" fear language. The report does the analysis; the email reports it factually.
- No countdown timers or scarcity tactics. 4-7.15 forbids unduly manipulative content.
- No contingency fee promises in writing.
