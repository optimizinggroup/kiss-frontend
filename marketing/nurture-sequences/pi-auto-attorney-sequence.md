1. Florida is a no-fault state, which means your own PIP coverage pays for your medical bills

**Sent:** 7 days after Email 1.
**Subject:** `Three things to keep in your glovebox, {{contact_name}}`
**Preheader:** `If you ever get into an accident, you'll be glad you read this`

```
Hi {{contact_name}},

Quick follow-up to your policy review last week.

If you ever get into a car accident in Florida — and statistically about one in seven drivers
will this year — what you have on you in the first ten minutes can shape what happens for the
next year.

Three things to keep in your glovebox or in your phone:

1. A laminated card with your insurance company name, policy number, and 24-hour claims phone.
   Pull this up before you call your insurer at the scene — they'll ask for the policy number
   first thing, and most people fumble.

2. A small notepad and pen. Cell phones break in collisions. A pen and pad always work. Write
   down the other driver's name, license plate, insurance card photo, and three witnesses'
   contact info if anyone saw it.

3. A note to yourself: "Call my own insurance first, not the other driver's." In Florida,
   because of PIP, your insurer pays your medical bills first regardless of fault. Don't let
   the other driver's adjuster — who is not on your side — take a recorded statement from you
   in the first 48 hours.

Bonus tip: take photos of every vehicle, the position of the cars, the road, and any visible
injuries. Photos taken in the first ten minutes are worth more than any later memory.

Hope you never need this. But if you do, you'll be glad you read it.

## Email 3 — Day 30 — "What to do in the first hour after a crash"

**Sent:** 30 days after Email 1.
**Subject:** `The first hour after a crash matters most`
**Preheader:** `A practical playbook from {{tenant.brand_name}}`

```
Hi {{contact_name}},

It's been about a month since your policy review. Here's something I wish every Florida driver
had pinned to their visor:

What to do in the first hour after a car accident — a step-by-step.

If anyone is hurt: Call 911 first. Always. Even if injuries seem minor — adrenaline hides a
lot. Get medical attention checked out within 14 days regardless, because Florida's PIP
statute requires you to seek treatment within that window or you may lose the benefit.

If everyone is okay:
1. Move to safety if cars are blocking traffic and drivable. If not, stay put and turn on
   hazards.
2. Call the police even on minor accidents. A police report is gold later. Florida law
   requires a report when there's injury, death, or property damage over $500 — but get one
   anyway.
3. Photograph: all four corners of both vehicles, license plates, the road and any skid marks,
   the position of the cars before they're moved, traffic signals or signs nearby, and any
   visible injuries (yourself or your passengers).
4. Exchange: name, phone, license, insurance card photo. Do NOT discuss fault. Do NOT
   apologize, even reflexively — anything you say can be used against your claim.
5. Call YOUR insurance company. Not theirs.
6. Save everything. Photos, dashcam footage, witness contacts. Don't post anything on social
   media — adjusters check.

What NOT to do in the first 48 hours:
- Don't give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurance company. They will call.
  Politely decline. You are not obligated.
- Don't sign anything from any insurance company without reading every word.
- Don't accept a quick settlement. Initial offers are almost always low; injuries from car
  accidents often surface in the first weeks, not the first hours.

If you're ever in a situation where you'd like a second pair of eyes on whether an offer is
fair, that's what we're here for. Free consult, no obligation. Reply to this email or call
{{tenant.phone}}.

— {{tenant.contact_name}}
{{tenant.brand_name}}

## Email 4 — Day 60 — "Hidden gaps in Florida auto coverage"

**Sent:** 60 days after Email 1.
**Subject:** `5 hidden gaps Florida drivers don't know they have`
**Preheader:** `What your insurance company hopes you never read`

```
Hi {{contact_name}},

Following up from your policy review two months ago.

Most Florida drivers have at least one of these gaps in their auto coverage — and most don't
find out until after a crash. Some of these may have been called out in your original review,
some may not.

1. UM / UIM at low limits or stacked-rejected.
   Uninsured Motorist is what protects you when the other driver has minimum coverage or none.
   Florida has one of the highest uninsured driver rates in the country. If you have a $10K
   bodily injury policy and you're hit by an uninsured driver, you have $10K to cover medical
   bills, lost wages, pain and suffering, and everything else. UM stacking, an FL-specific
   feature, can multiply this across vehicles on your policy for a small premium increase.

2. PIP only at the $10K minimum.
   Florida's no-fault law requires $10K of PIP. Most policies stop there. After your medical
   bills hit $10K — which happens fast with even a moderate ER visit — your health insurance
   takes over (with deductibles and copays you'd otherwise avoid).

3. No MedPay coverage.
   MedPay is separate from PIP and pays for medical expenses regardless of fault, even on top
   of PIP. It's typically $5-15/month for $5,000 of coverage. The math almost always favors
   adding it.

4. Rental coverage missing or low.
   If your car is in the body shop for two weeks, who pays for the rental? If your policy
   doesn't include rental reimbursement (or has a $30/day cap on a $60/day rental), that's out
   of pocket.

5. Liability limits at FL minimums.
   Florida requires only $10K of property damage and $10K of PIP. Bodily injury liability is
   technically not required (though most lenders require it). If you cause an accident with
   serious injuries and you have only minimum coverage, your personal assets — your home, your
   wages — are exposed.

If you'd like to talk through any of these, just reply. I'd rather you ask now than wish you
had asked later.

— {{tenant.contact_name}}
{{tenant.brand_name}}

[Compliance footer]
```

**Why this email:** Educational, valuable content. The 5 gaps are universal in FL auto. The CTA is soft — reply to ask. Avoids overpromising or fear-based language (which would violate Rule 4-7.15).

## Operational notes for Make implementation

- **Trigger:** Email 1 fires inside the Make scenario (module 14 in v2 blueprint). Emails 2-5 fire from a SECOND scenario triggered by a daily timer that queries Supabase for `submissions` rows where `email_sequence_step < 5` AND the appropriate days have elapsed.
- **Scheduling table:** add an `email_schedule` table (or columns on `submissions`) tracking `step_completed`, `next_send_at`. Easier to add later than retrofit.
- **Unsubscribe handling:** Resend natively handles list-unsubscribe headers. Configure once at the Resend domain level and every email in the sequence inherits it. Maintain a `consent_status = unsubscribed` flag on the submission row so Make filters it out before sending.
- **A/B testing:** keep the subject lines in a Supabase table once you want to iterate. For launch, ship as-is.
- **Bar filing:** all 5 emails should be filed with FL Bar Ethics & Advertising Dept before activation per Rule 4-7.19. See `florida-bar-compliance.md`.

## What this sequence does NOT include (intentional)

- No outbound phone-call asks. Email-only is the safest channel under 4-7.18.
- No price promises ("we don't get paid unless you do"). Even though that's typical PI fee structure, putting it in writing in a marketing email is risky territory under 4-7.13.
- No specific case results. Bar rules around past results are tight in FL.
- No comparisons to other firms.
- No countdown timers or fake urgency. 4-7.15 prohibits unduly manipulative content.
